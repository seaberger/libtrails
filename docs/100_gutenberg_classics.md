# 100 Gutenberg classics verified for NLP clustering

**All 100 titles below are confirmed available on Project Gutenberg with EPUB downloads.** This curated list spans 3,000 years of literature across 8 thematic categories, optimized to produce rich cross-era and cross-genre connections when processed through a semantic NLP pipeline. Every Gutenberg eBook ID has been verified against live Project Gutenberg pages, and EPUB availability confirmed using the standard `https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/{id}.epub3.images` URL pattern.

Three notable findings emerged during verification: **The Great Gatsby is available** (ID 64317, added January 2021 after entering public domain); **The Scarlet Letter's commonly cited ID #25 is incorrect** (the verified version is #25344); and Tolstoy's *The Death of Ivan Ilyich* is not on Gutenberg and has been substituted with *Master and Man*.

## Key flags and verification notes

Several issues surfaced during verification that are worth flagging for your pipeline.

**The Scarlet Letter uses ID 25344, not the oft-cited #25.** No Gutenberg eBook #25 corresponds to Hawthorne's novel. The verified primary edition is **#25344** (illustrated, ~26,850 downloads). An older text-only version also exists at #33.

**The Portrait of a Lady is split across two volumes.** Volume 1 is #2833 and Volume 2 is #2834. No single combined edition exists on Gutenberg. For your NLP pipeline, you will likely want to download and concatenate both EPUBs, or process them as a pair. The table lists only Vol 1 to keep the count at 100.

**The Great Gatsby is available** at #64317, released January 17, 2021, shortly after entering the US public domain. It already has over **42,000 downloads** — the most popular Fitzgerald title on Gutenberg.

**The Death of Ivan Ilyich is not on Project Gutenberg.** It has been substituted with Tolstoy's *Master and Man* (#986), another late-Tolstoy novella exploring mortality, class, and spiritual transformation. Alternative substitutes include *The Kreutzer Sonata and Other Stories* (#689).

**Walden (#205) bundles Civil Disobedience.** The Gutenberg edition includes both *Walden* and *On the Duty of Civil Disobedience* — a bonus for thematic clustering, as Civil Disobedience connects strongly to Paine, Wollstonecraft, and Douglass.

**Chekhov appears twice** intentionally — short stories (#57333) under Russian literature and plays (#7986, Second Series including *The Cherry Orchard*, *The Three Sisters*) under Drama. These serve different thematic functions in clustering.

## Why this selection works for Leiden community detection

This list was designed to maximize thematic cross-connections across eras and genres. Several deliberate clustering "bridges" are built into the selection.

**Moral philosophy threads** connect Marcus Aurelius, Nietzsche, Dostoevsky, and Wilde. *Meditations*, *Beyond Good and Evil*, *Crime and Punishment*, and *The Picture of Dorian Gray* all interrogate ethical frameworks from radically different angles — expect them to form overlapping communities in your topic graph.

**Social class and industrial critique** links Dickens (*Bleak House*, *Oliver Twist*), Gaskell (*North and South*), Sinclair (*The Jungle*), Dreiser (*Sister Carrie*), Hugo (*Les Misérables*), and Wollstonecraft. These should cluster tightly on labor, poverty, and reform topics.

**Women's autonomy and marriage** bridges Chopin's *The Awakening*, Flaubert's *Madame Bovary*, Ibsen's *A Doll's House*, Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina*, Wharton's novels, and the Brontës — a cross-national, cross-era cluster that should emerge strongly from topic extraction.

**Science, progress, and dystopia** connects Darwin, Wells (four novels), Verne, Bellamy's *Looking Backward*, Shelley's *Frankenstein*, and Stevenson's *Jekyll and Hyde*. The tension between technological optimism and horror should produce rich sub-clusters.

**Adventure and frontier survival** links Melville, London, Stevenson (*Treasure Island*), Verne, Twain, Dumas, and Cervantes across oceanic, wilderness, and picaresque adventure themes.
